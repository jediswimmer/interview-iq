## THE 10 QUESTIONS EVAN WILL MOST LIKELY ASK

### 1. "Walk me through your background."
**Goal:** 90 seconds, not 5 minutes. Hit the narrative arc.

> "I started at Microsoft in San Diego — spent four years there, got recognized by Ballmer and the board for store launch contributions. That's where I learned the Microsoft ecosystem inside and out. Moved to Softchoice where I got my first team leadership role — 20 account executives selling Enterprise Agreements. Then Insight acquired PCM while I was there, and I rode that transition from cloud solution sales to team lead. That's where I grew Microsoft revenue 195% and won the Product Management MVP. AWS recruited me to run the Microsoft workloads GTM team — that was my window into how the other side of the OEM relationship works, and I contributed to a business doing $680M in ARR. Now I'm at Dynapt, where I run a 36-person org across three teams, own our partnerships with Microsoft, AWS, Ingram Micro, and Pax8, and I'm building our AI practice from the ground up. I'm looking to take the next step into a VP role where I can do what I've been doing at Dynapt — but at real scale."

### 2. "Your title is Director. Why are you right for a VP role?"
**This is THE question. Nail it.**

> "At Dynapt, my title says Director but my scope says VP. I run 36+ people across three teams — infrastructure, managed services, and AI development. I report directly to the CEO. I own four OEM relationships personally. I manage the funding models, the co-sell programs, the certification compliance. The title is a reflection of the company's size, not the scope of the work. What I'm looking for is an organization where the title matches the job I'm already doing."

### 3. "Tell me about your OEM partnership experience."
**Map directly to the JD.**

> "I own the relationships with Microsoft, AWS, Ingram Micro, and Pax8 at Dynapt. That means I manage the co-sell motions, handle program governance and certification compliance, negotiate MDF and funding models, and drive joint go-to-market with each of them. But what makes my experience different is that I've also been inside the OEMs. I was a direct Microsoft employee for four years and an AWS employee for the Microsoft workloads team. So I understand how the partner programs work from the inside — what drives partner investment decisions, how co-sell is actually executed at the field level, what makes a partner relationship productive vs. transactional. Most partnership leaders have worked one side. I've worked both."

### 4. "Your AWS tenure was only 15 months. What happened?"
**Have a clean, honest answer.**

> "AWS was a fantastic experience — I got to operate at $680M ARR scale and see how a world-class partner ecosystem runs from the inside. But the role was a specialist position focused specifically on Microsoft workloads in SMB. After 15 months, I'd accomplished what I was brought in to do — built the readiness programs, set up the GTM motions, established the partner pipeline model. When the opportunity came to build something from scratch at Dynapt — to actually own a team, own partnerships, own the strategy — that was the next step I wanted. At AWS I was executing. At Dynapt I'm building."

### 5. "How would you transition from a 50-person company to a 5,000+ person org?"
**Show you've operated at scale before.**

> "I've worked at both ends. Microsoft was a massive org. AWS was massive. Insight was an $8 billion company. I know how larger organizations move — the cross-functional alignment, the stakeholder management, the governance layers. At Dynapt I had to build all of that from scratch because it didn't exist. In a larger org, the infrastructure exists — my job would be to optimize it, not create it. If anything, going from building a partner program with zero infrastructure to managing one with established systems and teams is a step toward leverage, not complexity."

### 6. "What's your experience with PE-backed companies?"
**Be honest. Spin the upside.**

> "I haven't worked in a PE portfolio company before — this would be my first. But I've spent my career driving the kind of results PE firms care about: revenue growth, partner-sourced pipeline, operational efficiency. At Dynapt, I grew professional services demand 10x with limited resources. At Insight, 195% Microsoft revenue growth. At AWS, contributing to $680M ARR. I'm used to aggressive targets and accountability. What I bring to a PE environment is that builder mentality — I've done more with less, which means with real PE-level resources and structure, I can move even faster."

### 7. "Tell me about your AI experience."
**This is your differentiator. Don't oversell, but don't undersell either.**

> "I run an 8-person AI development team at Dynapt. We've built production agentic AI systems — multi-agent orchestration, retrieval-augmented generation, knowledge graph architectures. I've taken GenAI pilots from concept to production in legal, healthcare, and retail. The automation platforms we built won both the Microsoft and AWS Shark Tank competitions. This isn't theoretical for me — I'm hands-on with LLMs, prompt engineering, and intelligent document processing. For a technology services company, having a partnerships leader who actually understands AI at a technical level is a significant advantage. I can speak the language with engineering teams, with product teams, and with customers."

### 8. "What attracted you to this role?"
**Be specific to the JD, not generic.**

> "Three things. First, the scope — owning OEM and strategic partner relationships at a $1B+ company is exactly the scale I'm ready for. Second, the PE angle — I'm drawn to the accountability and growth orientation that comes with PE ownership. And third, the timing — this is a secondary buyout to a Tier 1 PE firm, which means the new sponsors will want to accelerate. That's when the partnerships function becomes critical — it's how you expand market reach, drive revenue acceleration, and create differentiation. I want to be the person building that."

### 9. "What's your management style?"
**Ground it in specifics.**

> "I run three different teams with three very different profiles — engineers, managed services operators, and AI developers. So my style has to flex. With the engineering team, I'm more technical and collaborative — they need me to understand the architecture to make good partnership decisions. With managed services, it's operational — SLAs, staffing, escalation paths. With the AI team, it's more entrepreneurial — we're building new capabilities, so I give them room to experiment but keep them tied to client outcomes. Across all three, the constant is clarity — everyone knows what we're building, why, and how their work connects to revenue."

### 10. "What questions do you have for us?"
**Always have 3-4. These signal how you think.**

> 1. "What does the partnership portfolio look like today? How many OEM relationships, and what's the current state of those programs?"
> 2. "The JD mentions the company is going through a secondary buyout to a Tier 1 PE firm. How far along is that transition, and what does the new sponsor's value creation thesis look like for the partnerships function specifically?"
> 3. "What does the team look like that this role would inherit? Is it building from scratch or optimizing an existing structure?"
> 4. "What does success look like in the first 12 months? What would make the PE sponsor say 'we made the right hire'?"

## TACTICAL NOTES

### Things to Remember About Evan Specifically
- He's academic background (UCLA PhD program, Fulbright). He'll appreciate structured, well-reasoned answers over folksy stories.
- He's a Project Manager, not a Senior Director. He's screening, not deep-assessing. Keep answers concise. Don't over-share.
- He's likely using a rubric tied to the JD. Hit the keywords naturally: partnership plan execution, field sales alignment, program governance, certification compliance, offering innovation, funding forecasting.
- He's probably evaluating 5-10 candidates this week. Make his job easy — be clear, be specific, be memorable.

### Body Language / Delivery (if video)
- Camera on, good lighting, clean background
- Lean in when answering
- Don't read notes — glance at bullet points
- Smile when talking about your wins. Executives who enjoy their work are more compelling.

### Things NOT to Say
- Don't trash Dynapt or Greg. "I love what I've built at Dynapt" is the right frame.
- Don't say "I'm looking for more money." Say "I'm looking for scale."
- Don't volunteer the short tenures. If he asks, use the prepared answers. If he doesn't, don't bring it up.
- Don't ask about comp on this call. That's Stage 3/4.

### The One Line to Land
If you only get one moment to be memorable, make it this:

> "I've been on both sides of the OEM relationship — inside Microsoft, inside AWS, and on the partner side at Insight and Dynapt. Most people in this role have worked one side. I've worked both. And I'm building production AI systems alongside the partnership work. That combination is hard to find."
